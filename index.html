How socioeconomic class effects education for minorities/POC

Education disparities are a severe issue that applies to millions of people to this day and will continue to have long-lasting and generational effects. It is important to spread awareness to minimize the disproportionate learning losses for low-income students of color.

About

The socioeconomic divide is really defined by who can pursue higher education and who cannot. Elite schools are mainly attended by high-income students even though academic achievement is evenly distributed, regardless of income. Those with high income attend better funded schools with more resources and recognition that makes a difference on college applications. Additionally, those with lower incomes may be discouraged from pursuing higher education due to the high and rising costs of tuition. Ultimately, income can be a leading factor in higher education and will only continue to increase the socioeconomic divide, as those with college degrees have an advantage when pursuing jobs in the future. This is important because everyone should have a fair and equal opportunity to learn and receive an education–nobody should have to sacrifice that as a means of survival. There are many people who don’t think about the issue regarding socioeconomic status and education. It’s important to make this known so people can realize that not everyone has the same opportunities. Giving low economic status families with children a chance to further that child’s education is very important.
We know that income is very influential, but there are many factors that can influence income. With wage gaps, not only is it an influence on income, but it also emphasizes that there are disparities on various levels when we talk about income and education. It is well known that minorities by race and gender receive less to the dollar of their white, male counterparts, with the rate of the gap differing by each race and gender/race intertwined. For example, women will receive less than their white male counterparts, but women of color will receive even less than their white female counterparts. And asian women may get paid more than their black female coworkers, etc… Not only are those with low-income less likely to have an equal opportunity for higher education but minorities/POC may be more negatively affected and feel this inequality at a more severe level. This is just as important because gender and race, something we are born with, should not be accepted in our society as an obstacle preventing us from education or obtaining a job.
The college admissions process is arguably the cause and a solution to this disparity. College applications can advantage those with higher income, but can also be used to set an equal field for all students. In recent years, affirmative action and the authority of school boards to shift the evaluation process has been prioritized, especially regarding people of color. However, schools can also take income into consideration and evaluate all the ways in which it can affect the application process/resume of students. Some other organizations have been finding other means to make a difference, starting with the students. There are multiple non-profit organizations across America that work to provide tutoring and other resources/scholarship opportunities for low-income students for free. Any of us can donate to these organizations easily from our phones.\

Children Left Behind

It is common for Children in low socioeconomic households to be “left behind” in school. It is not uncommon for a child to fall behind in a subject or specific class. However, when a child falls behind it takes extra time, attention, effort, and resources to bring that child back to where they need to be. Some children also have natural learning deficiencies, such as a deficiency in reading, speech, and or math. These deficiencies usually require specialized help and attention in at least the area the child struggles in. However, if a child's school does not offer programs to support and help the child, then the child and their family will be left to seek a form of outside help. However, some parents can not afford to seek out and get their children this extra support, or can't afford to move their children to a school where these programs are already implemented. Without the proper help, these children will most likely struggle very early on in school, and continually fall behind as their education goes on. Most schools in low socioeconomic areas are missing these programs, and have almost no way to offer their students any specialized support. However, this is an issue that can be solved. By increasing funding for schools, especially those in low socioeconomic areas, we can give schools the means to add much-needed support programs. You can vote for and support bills to increase school funding, and if you have the means you can even donate directly to school programs, these are all ways you make a difference. We need to support all children and make the help they need available and affordable.

Disparities Among Minorities

Cultural differences in the ways that children are spoken to and raised changes the outcome of development in non-"one size fits all" curriculums and education systems. For instance, linguistic and cultural differences such as different languages or ways of being raised can drastically affect the rate at which students learn at school, as it is hard to adapt to a new culture/environment and a new language. Another thing that school systems, usually the ones that cater towards a certain set demographic, do not account for is the mental and social wellbeing of minority students, as they sometimes have them feeling left out or do not cater to and have tolerances for different cultures. The school systems who do cater to minority demographics as well, however, encourage duality between student's "main" language and their home-learned/spoken language, thus allowing for more cultural acceptance and diversity at school. This has also been shown to result in more of an increase in educational statistics for minority students in these tolerant school systems compared to minorities in non-culturally tolerant systems, thus showing the importance of the formerly mentioned school systems.
